\section{The Big Four: Modeling Burden, Problem Dimensionality, System Design, and Usability}
In line with the state of the art, my prior work reveals insights across four critical dimensions that govern robotic manipulation research. I also discuss the potential of learning-based approaches in addressing some of these challenges.

\header{Modeling Burden} While embodiment-driven analytical models provide theoretical foundations, they often fall short when modeling complex interactions. The progression from simulation-in-the-loop approaches (PokeRRT \cite{pasricha2022pokerrt}) to transformer-based dynamics models \cite{reed2024transformer} illustrates that learning-based methods have the potential to model non-linear and stochastic phenomena across diverse real-world settings. However, the effectiveness of these models remains highly task-dependent, as evidenced by the specialized constraints needed for liquid handling \cite{abderezaei2024clutter} versus whole-body manipulation under joint failures \cite{briscoe2024exploring}. Additionally, model fidelity and speed inform planning success across the high-dimensional search space of robot-object interactions in the real-world.

\header{Problem Dimensionality}
This search space involves the consideration of higher-order states like velocities, accelerations, and forces for dynamic motions, in addition to geometric constraints like positional limits and obstacle avoidance \cite{schmerling2019kinodynamic}. Finding good initial solutions that can then be optimized \cite{kuntz2019fast}, pre-computation in the combined robot and task space \cite{shome2021asymptotically,briscoe2024exploring}, and lazy approaches to dynamics propagation and collision checking \cite{pasricha2024virtues} can begin to address computational tractability issues especially as they arise from imperfect models in simple environments \cite{pasricha2022pokerrt,briscoe2024exploring,abderezaei2024clutter}.
However, even with recent advances in parallel collision-checking and forward kinematics calculations \cite{sundaralingam2023curobo,thomason2024motions}, computational complexity and memory consumption remain prohibitive for high-dimensional problems due to the inherent difficulty of generating valid samples, approximating distances in non-Euclidean spaces, and connecting states with dynamically feasible trajectories.
Learning-based approaches provide an alternative solution by implicitly compressing high-dimensional spaces through learned representations, which enables efficient and complex reasoning in state-action spaces \cite{qureshi2020motion}.

The multi-query kinodynamic planner with lazy evaluation techniques represents an intermediate solution that improves computation time but remains fundamentally limited compared to generative approaches.

\header{System Design}
While traditional robotic planning systems offer verifiable safety assurance, explainable components for debugging, and generalizability to different scenarios, they face significant integration challenges and require extensive engineering effort when combining multiple specialized components \cite{abderezaei2024clutter}. Each component requires careful tuning, creating complex error propagation through multiple components, resulting in brittleness at system boundaries. Moreover, system performance typically does not improve without manual intervention and overall processing speed is subject to high variance depending on component choice and tuning.
Learning-based approaches offer advantages through end-to-end trainable architectures that naturally handle component interactions, improving scalability to new tasks without requiring extensive redesign as well as lending themselves to progressive improvement over the course of system deployment.

\section{Diffusion Models}

Diffusion models have recently emerged as an effective generative modeling approach for producing high-quality, diverse samples from learned approximations to conditional or unconditional \textsl{multimodal} distributions, albeit at the cost of longer generation times \cite{sohl2015deep,ho2020denoising,rombach2022high,nichol2021glide}.
% Their key strength lies in capturing multimodality---the ability to effectively model conditional or unconditional multimodal distributions of the underlying dataset \cite{chi2023diffusion,sridhar2024nomad}.

\header{Background}
Diffusion operates as a two-step process: forward and reverse (also known as inference, sampling, planning, or denoising). In its simplest implementation, the training process consists of adding noise according to a scheduler to a training data sample, then training a neural architecture to predict the magnitude of added noise (epsilon prediction), and minimizing the MSE loss between the added noise and the predicted noise \cite{ho2020denoising}.
During inference, random Gaussian noise of the same dimensionality as the target data is generated and iteratively denoised through the trained neural architecture over multiple steps to generate a valid sample.
This class of generative methods has found application in robotics in two main areas: imitation learning \cite{chi2023diffusion,sridhar2024nomad} and seeding for trajectory optimization \cite{carvalho2023motion,huang2024diffusionseeder}, owing to its ability to represent multimodal distributions effectively and produce diverse and high-quality samples from the modeled data space.
While existing applications have shown diverse real-world behaviors through end-effector delta actions, they primarily focus on kinematic constraints, require many human demonstrations, and necessitate training a new model for novel behaviors \cite{chi2023diffusion,ajay2023is,ha2023scaling,ze20243d}. These limitations direct my research toward the seeding problem for trajectory optimization---specifically, developing diffusion models that produce kinematically and dynamically admissible trajectories which can further be refined by standard trajectory optimization iterations.
% that respect both kinematic and dynamic constraints, thereby enabling enhanced capabilities for robotic manipulation without extensive demonstration datasets.

\header{Formalization}
A joint space trajectory with $t$ waypoints at denoising step $k$ is defined as
$\bm{\pi}^k = [ X_0^k, X_1^k, \dots, X_{t-1}^k ]^T$
where $X_i = (q_i, \dot{q}_i, \ddot{q}_i) \in \mathbb{R}^n$ represents the complete state (joint angles, velocities, and accelerations) of an $n$-DoF robot arm at timestep $i$.
The training process for the diffusion model approximates the conditional distribution $p(\bm{\pi}\ |\ \mathcal{C})$, where $\mathcal{C}$ is the target conditioning embedding (\textcolor{customred}{\Cref{fig:diffusion}}).
It samples $\bm{\pi}$ from dataset $\mathcal{D}$, adds noise according to a schedule parameterized by $\alpha$, $\gamma$, and $\sigma$, and learns to predict the underlying sample. The noise prediction network $\epsilon_\theta$, where $\theta$ represents the parameters of the 1D U-Net architecture, is trained to minimize
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta) = MSE(\epsilon^k, \epsilon_\theta(\mathcal{C}, \bm{\pi}^0 + \epsilon^k, k))
\end{equation}

Given a trained $\epsilon_\theta$, the $K$-step iterative denoising process
\begin{equation}
    \bm{\pi}^{k-1} = \alpha \cdot (\bm{\pi}^k - \gamma\epsilon_\theta(\mathcal{C}, \bm{\pi}^k, k) + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2I))
\end{equation}
starts from $\bm{\pi}^K \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and generates a trajectory $\bm{\pi}^0$ that respects the conditioning information and obeys start, goal, joint limit, collision, and dynamic constraints.
In practice, start and goal states are enforced via inpainting \cite{carvalho2023motion}, i.e., terminal states are ignored from the denoising process and set to problem constraints $\bm{\pi}_0^k = X_{start}$ and $\bm{\pi}_{t-1}^k = X_{goal}$ where $\dot{q}_{start} = \dot{q}_{goal} = \ddot{q}_{start} = \ddot{q}_{goal} = \bm{0}$.
Collisions are avoided via inference-time gradient guidance $\bm{\pi}^{k-1} = \bm{\pi}^k - \beta\ \cdot \nabla J(\bm{\pi}; E)$ where $\beta$ is a tunable guidance weight, $J$ defines the collision cost function and $E$ represents the set of all obstacles in the environment \cite{saha2024edmp}.
Additionally, joint limits are enforced via clamping during training and inference.

% Specific to our work, $p(\bm{\pi}\ |\ \mathcal{C})$ models system dynamics through a conditioning mechanism that appends the target conditioning embedding $m$ to the diffusion timestep embedding in the noise prediction network architecture \cite{chi2023diffusionpolicy}. This global conditioning influences the entire trajectory, thereby ensuring dynamic feasibility.

\header{Building Blocks}
The diffusion-based trajectory generation system comprises four core components (\cref{fig:diffusion}):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{\textcolor{customblack}{Unconditional Backbone}:} The foundational block is an unconditional diffusion model that generates samples from Gaussian noise. The denoising architecture can be selected based on task complexity and data characteristics. For instance, transformers may be preferable for high-rate actions, though CNN-based architectures remain effective for positioning tasks \cite{chi2023diffusion,seo2024presto,peebles2023scalable,carvalho2023motion,janner2022planning}. When trained on a diverse set robot trajectories, this backbone can generate approximate trajectories that serve as effective starting points for optimization-based methods. Improving these initial approximations can significantly reduce subsequent optimization iterations.
    
    \item \textbf{\textcolor{customgreen}{Data-Driven Refinement}:} This unconditional approximation can be enhanced to better capture the underlying distribution through high-quality, task-specific training data. The model can be also be guided toward on-manifold learning through task-specific loss function design, in addition to the standard noise prediction loss \cite{chi2023diffusion,saha2024edmp,carvalho2023motion}. Examples of loss functions include trajectory smoothness and collision distance \cite{seo2024presto}, and Cartesian space error and non-linearity-aware weighting \cite{huang2024diffusionseeder}. These design choices can be supplemented by the numerous methods available in machine learning literature to support generalization (e.g., regularization, data augmentation, cross-validation).
    
    \item \textbf{\textcolor{customaqua}{Inference-Time Cost Guidance}:} A distinctive advantage of diffusion models is the ability to apply cost guidance \textsl{at inference time}, steering the denoising process to generate trajectories that avoid obstacles and respect higher-order constraints. This approach requires diversity in training data to function effectively. Additionally, inpainting techniques enforce hard state compliance for problem-specific constraints like start and goal states, while the remainder of the trajectory is generated through the denoising process \cite{janner2022planning,saha2024edmp,carvalho2023motion}.
    
    \item \textbf{\textcolor{customred}{Constraint Integration}:} Real-world tasks invariably operate under both kinematic and dynamic constraints. These constraints are incorporated via the conditioning mechanism, effectively learning a conditional distribution that leverages prior work in sensory feature learning \cite{krizhevsky2012imagenet}. This conditioning can take various forms---RGB images \cite{chi2023diffusion,sridhar2024nomad}, point clouds \cite{ze20243d}, depth information \cite{huang2024diffusionseeder}, or task-centric representations \cite{seo2024presto}---all aimed at generating near-admissible motions that can be further refined by trajectory optimization.
\end{enumerate}


While these building blocks provide a foundation for trajectory generation, existing diffusion-based approaches primarily focus on kinematic feasibility and collision avoidance, overlooking the critical role of dynamics constraints in real-world robotic tasks. This limitation significantly reduces their effectiveness as initializers for trajectory optimizers like GOMP-FIT \cite{ichnowski2022gomp}, which must subsequently reconcile the kinematically-viable but dynamically-infeasible paths.

My current and proposed work address the fundamental challenge of incorporating dynamic constraints directly into the diffusion model framework. Rather than treating diffusion models as geometric path planners, I present a current work that uses a high-level conditioning mechanism to implicitly encode dynamic constraints for a payload transport task and I propose a follow-up work that presents a task-agnostic trajectory generation framework.
As a result, this work aims to significantly reduce the computation burden on trajectory optimization with kinematic and dynamic constraints.

\header{Trajectory Generation with Implicit Dynamics Modeling}
To overcome the challenges of handling coupled robot-object dynamics in high-dimensional state spaces, my current research explores generative diffusion models for trajectory planning that implicitly incorporate dynamic constraints \cite{pasricha2024towards,wang2001payload}.
As a specific use case of this approach and without loss of generalization, I focus on expanding the operational capabilities of existing robot hardware by enabling super-nominal payload manipulation---allowing safe operation beyond manufacturer-specified limits while respecting hardware constraints.
% This work significantly expands the operational capabilities of existing robot hardware by enabling super-nominal payload manipulation---allowing robots to safely operate beyond manufacturer-specified limits while respecting true hardware constraints.
% By simultaneously modeling both kinematic and dynamic constraints, the system properly accounts for how payload characteristics interact with joint configurations and their higher-order derivatives to determine feasible motions.

Diffusion models offer a compelling solution to this challenge through their inherent properties: they naturally explore diverse solution trajectories through stochastic denoising \cite{chi2023diffusion} while modeling complex, multimodal distributions that accommodate rich contextual information. My key innovation extends beyond existing diffusion-based approaches that operate solely in position space \cite{carvalho2023motion,saha2024edmp} by jointly learning valid distributions across positions, velocities, and accelerations, thereby addressing the complete dynamic state space.

Specifically, we extend the diffusion policy architecture to condition trajectory generation on payload mass through global conditioning \cite{chi2023diffusion}. Unlike local conditioning where features influence each timestep individually, global conditioning applies the mass embedding uniformly across the entire trajectory generation process, preserving dynamic feasibility across the generated motion (\Cref{fig:architecture}).
Through training on dynamically verified trajectories, the model implicitly encodes kinodynamic constraints without requiring explicit mathematical formulation during inference. Our experimental results demonstrate that robots can safely handle payloads across $67.6\%$ of the nominal workspace at $3\times$ rated capacity while maintaining sub-$0.1$ second planning times.

The framework currently lacks cross-task generalization, being primarily trained for tabletop manipulation. This specificity limits applicability to arbitrary point-to-point motions and more complex task spaces.
The experiments were conducted in relatively collision-sparse environments with simplifying assumptions about payload characteristics, particularly regarding mass distribution.
